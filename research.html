Research

Lynn Lab: First Semester

In the Spring of 2017, I began working with Professor Theresa Lynn in her Quantum Information Theory Lab. I continued a project that's been going on for about a decade: looking at the maximum distinguishability of Bell bases using Linear Evolution Local Measurement (LELM) devices. It's shown in the paper "Methods for Reliable Transportation" that LELM devices cannot distinguish all four of the Bell basis elements for two entangled two-dimensional particles. The question we ask is, for two entangled n-dimensional particles, how many of the n^2 Bell basis elements can we distinguish using LELM devices? My first semester of work for the project can be found here: "Lynn Lab: General Tools for Distinguishability of Bell States Using LELM Devices". I really enjoy this project because it lies at the perfect intersections. I'm able to think algorithmically, apply group theory and linear algebra, and have my results be physically interpretable!

Building A Better Minesweeper

In the Fall of 2017, I worked with Professor Mohamed Omar to build and analyze variations of the game Minesweeper. My work culminated in this paper: "Gridsweeper: NP-Completeness of Variants to the Minesweeper Solvability Problem", in which I analyze a variation of Minesweeper played on a grid, and prove that a decision problem related to that game is NP-Complete. As a corollary, we get that the same decision problem for traditional Minesweeper is NP-Complete.
